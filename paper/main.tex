\begin{abstract}
Kyrgyz, a Turkic language with roughly five million speakers, is nearly absent from
large language model evaluation. Where low-resource Turkic languages are measured at
all, the evidence is usually a translation score on a machine-translated test set,
which conflates the model with the translation system used to build the test.
We introduce a native-speaker-authored benchmark of \textbf{TODO-N} four-option items
covering eight linguistic phenomena, from vowel harmony and agglutinative suffix
stacking to idioms, orthography, and culturally grounded knowledge. Scoring is
deterministic and every result is tested against a 25\% random baseline.
Evaluating \textbf{TODO-M} models, we find a consistent dissociation: models answer
factual and lexical questions at or near ceiling while dropping sharply on items that
require \emph{producing} correct Kyrgyz morphology, with the same score for a small
and a large model from the same family. We then show that this gap is not intrinsic:
fine-tuning on rule-generated inflection data raises held-out morphological accuracy
from \textbf{TODO}\% to \textbf{TODO}\%. We release the benchmark, the rule engine,
and all code.
\end{abstract}

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\paragraph{Models.} TODO: list models, versions, and access dates.

\paragraph{Prompting.} Items are presented in Kyrgyz with numbered options and the
model is asked to reply with a single digit. Numeric labels avoid Latin/Cyrillic
homoglyph ambiguity between \texttt{A} and \foreignlanguage{russian}{А}.

\paragraph{Scoring.} A reply is parsed to an option index, first by a bare digit and
then by exact match against option text. Replies yielding neither are recorded as
\emph{unparseable}, counted as incorrect, and reported separately, since failure to
follow the format is a different failure from answering wrongly.

\paragraph{Statistics.} Accuracy is compared against the 25\% chance level with a
one-sided binomial test, overall and per model $\times$ category.

\section{Closing the Gap by Fine-Tuning}
\label{sec:finetune}

\subsection{Rule-generated data}

We implement Kyrgyz noun inflection as an explicit rule engine covering four-way
vowel harmony, consonant assimilation of suffix onsets, final-obstruent voicing, and
the orthographic realisation of glide--vowel sequences. Notably, rounding harmony is
asymmetric: a low suffix vowel is rounded after mid \foreignlanguage{russian}{о} but
not after high \foreignlanguage{russian}{у}. The engine is unit-tested against the
hand-authored items, so the generated data inherits the native-verified forms.

\subsection{Setup and results}

Stems are split so that test stems never appear in training; any gain therefore
reflects generalisation of the rules rather than memorisation of forms.
